Abstract—Road safety strongly depends on the clear 
visibility of lane markings, especially under changing weather 
conditions. This project studies lane visibility distance using a
single forward- facing camera on urban and suburban roadsin
Slovakia. Instead of focusing only on lane detection accuracy, 
the work measures how far lane markings remain visible in 
real-world scenarios. Road data is collected under clear, rainy, 
foggy, and low-light conditions to examine environmental
impact. The captured videos are processed using image 
preprocessing and computer vision techniques to detect lane 
boundaries. The maximum visible lane length is then estimated 
and converted into real-world distance values. A comparative
analysis is performed between urban and suburban road 
environments. The study highlights how adverse weather 
conditions significantly reduce lane visibility distance. The 
results demonstrate that a low-cost monocular camera can 
provide meaningful visibility measurements. This work can 
contribute to improving driver assistance systems and 
intelligent transportation applications.

I. INTRODUCTION
Road safety is one of the most critical concerns in modern 
transportation systems, and driver visibility plays a major 
role in ensuring safe travel. Among various visual cues
available on the road, lane markings provide essential 
guidance for main- taining proper lane discipline, safe 
overtaking, and accurate steering control. However, the 
visibility of lane markings is highly influenced by 
environmental factors such as lighting conditions, road 
structure, and weather changes. In urban areas, factors like 
heavy traffic, shadows from buildings, and worn-out
markings can reduce clarity, while in suburban areas, longer 
road stretches and varying infrastructure introduce different 
visibility challenge Weather conditions such as rain, fog, and low-light environments further degrade lane visibility, making driving 
more risky. With the rapid development of Advanced
Driver Assistance Systems (ADAS), camera-based lane 
detection systems are increasingly used to support drivers. 
Although many existing studies focus on improving lane 
detection accuracy, limited attention has been given to 
measuring how far lane markings remain visible in realworld conditions. Understanding lane visibility distance 
is important because it directly relates to reaction time and 
driving safety. A reduced visibility distance can delay 
driver decisions and increase accident risk. Therefore, 
analyzing lane visibility in practical environments 
becomes essential for improving transportation safety 
systems.This project focuses on estimating lane visibility 
distance using a single forward-facing monocular camera 
on Slovak roadways. Unlike approaches that rely on 
expensive sensors such as LiDAR, this study emphasizes
a low-cost and practical camera-based solution. Data is 
collected from both urban and suburban roads under 
different weather conditions including clear, rainy, foggy, 
and low-light scenarios. The objective is to evaluate how 
environmental variations affect the maximum visible lane 
distance in real driving situations.The proposed system 
processes captured road videos using computer vision 
techniques such as image preprocessing, lane detection, 
and perspective correction. After detecting lane 
boundaries, the maximum visible lane length is calculated
and converted from pixel measurements to real-world
distance values. A comparative analysis is performed 
between road types and weather conditions to identify 
patterns in visibility reduction. The results help in 
understanding the limitations of camera-based systems 
under adverse conditions. This study contributes practical 
insights for enhancing ADAS and intelli- gent
transportation systems. By focusing on real-world Slovak 
road environments, the project aimsto provide meaningful
and region-specific analysis for improving road safety
II. LITERATURE SURVEY
Recent advancements in intelligent transportation systems 
and autonomous driving technologies have increased
research interest in lane detection and visibility analysis 
under varying environmental conditions. Accurate lane 
perception is essential for driver assistance systems and 
automated vehicles, as it directly affects vehicle positioning, 
lane keeping, and overall road safety. Several studies have 
explored different sensing techniques and machine learning 
approaches to improve lane detection reliability in realworld conditions.Galinski et al. 
[1] analyzed lane visibility distance on urban and suburban 
roads in Slovakia using a single-camera system under 
diverse weather conditions. Their study focused on 
understanding how environmental factors and road quality 
influence lane visibility. The researchers employed a singleframe road distance estimation method combined with 
rigorous data preprocessing techniques such as frame 
filtering and threshold-based validation to improve detection 
accuracy. Their analysis across 96 drives revealed that 
highways provide significantly better visibility compared to 
urban roads, with median visibility distances of 82.2 m on 
the D2 highway and 78.7 m on the D1 highway, compared 
to only 51.6 m in urban environments. The study also 
showed that weather conditions such as rain reduce lane 
visibility, highlighting the limitations and feasibility of lowcost monocular camera systems for real-time lane visibility 
estimation.Zhao et al.
[2] investigated methods to improve the reliability of 
LiDAR-based lane detection systems through the use of 
advanced road marking materials. Their research explored 
the effect of structured road markings coated with high 
refractive index glass beads to enhance LiDAR reflection 
performance. Experiments were conducted under five 
weather conditions including dry surfaces, wet roads, and 
varying rainfall intensities using both 128-layer and 16-layer 
LiDAR sensors. The findings demonstrated that structured 
road markings significantly improved LiDAR intensity 
readings and reduced signal attenuation over distance, 
particularly in dry conditions. The study emphasizes that 
improvements in road infrastructure materials can enhance 
sensing performance and reliability for autonomous vehicle 
navigation systems.Parate and Selvakumari 
[3] proposed AttBi-ResU, a smart 3D vision-based lane 
detection system designed to improve accuracy in complex 
driving environments. Their approach consists of a threestage pipeline including image preprocessing, feature 
extraction, and segmentation. In the preprocessing stage, 
RGB images are converted into grayscale and enhanced 
using bilateral entropy-based adaptive histogram 
equalization to improve contrast and reduce noise. The 
system then utilizes an attention-based Bidirectional Long 
Short-Term Memory (Bi-LSTM) network combined with a 
residual-dilation U-Net architecture for accurate 
spatiotemporal lane segmentation. Hyperparameters are 
optimized using an enhanced Krill Herd Optimization 
algorithm to minimize training loss and improve detection 
performance.Yang et al. 
[4] introduced StrikeNet, a deep convolutional LSTM-based 
framework for road lane reconstruction and lane keeping
control in autonomous vehicles. The proposed system 
integrates spatial learning from camera-based vision systems 
with temporal inference derived from in-vehicle sensor data. 
By combining sequential spatial outputs with Long ShortTerm Memory networks, StrikeNet can estimate reliable 
lane models even when lane markings are faint or absent. 
Experimental evaluations demonstrated significant 
improvements in vehicle control accuracy, reducing steering 
control errors by up to 72% and decreasing variance by as 
much as 94% compared with conventional 
approaches.Sultana et al.
[5] presented a robust vision-based lane detection and 
tracking system designed to operate under challenging 
environmental conditions such as low visibility, shadows, 
glare, and occlusion. Their approach introduced a 
Comprehensive Intensity Threshold Range (CITR) to 
enhance edge detection performance within the Canny 
operator. Additionally, a two-step geometric verification 
method using angle-based and length-based constraints was 
applied to eliminate false detections. A novel lane tracking 
algorithm was also developed to predict lane positions 
across frames using horizontal lane position ranges. 
Experimental evaluations using multiple datasets achieved 
an average detection accuracy of 97.55% with a processing 
speed of 22.33 ms per frame.
Overall, the reviewed literature demonstrates that lane 
detection and visibility estimation remain critical challenges 
for intelligent transportation systems, especially under 
varying weather conditions and complex road environments. 
While several approaches utilize LiDAR, deep learning 
models, and advanced sensing technologies, many of these 
solutions rely on expensive hardware or complex 
computational frameworks. Therefore, there is a growing 
need for efficient and low-cost camera-based systems 
capable of estimating lane visibility distance in real-world 
driving conditions. This research addresses this gap by 
developing a camera-based approach to analyze lane 
visibility distance on urban and suburban roadways under 
varying environmental conditions.
III. RESEARCH METHODOLOGY
This work presents a hybrid framework for estimating lane 
visibility distance using a monocular camera system under 
varying environmental conditions. The proposed 
methodology integrates computer vision techniques, 
attention-enhanced deep learning, and geometric constraint 
optimization to improve the robustness and accuracy of lane 
visibility estimation. The system processes road video 
frames captured from urban and suburban environments and 
estimates the maximum visible lane distance in meters.
Unlike traditional approaches that rely on static threshold 
filtering and single-frame geometric assumptions, the 
proposed system employs adaptive threshold learning and 
spatial-temporal feature refinement to improve detection 
reliability in complex scenarios such as rain, twilight, and 
mountainous roads. The overall architecture consists of 
three major stages: data preprocessing and normalization, 
attention-enhanced lane feature extraction, and geometric 
optimization for visibility estimation.
A. System Architecture
The architecture of the proposed framework follows a multistage processing pipeline designed to convert raw monocular 
camera frames into accurate lane visibility distance 
measurements.The pipeline begins with dataset input 
consisting of urban and suburban road frames captured using 
a forward-facing camera. The captured frames undergo 
preprocessing operations such as geometric correction, noise 
filtering, and contrast normalization to ensure consistent 
input quality. Following preprocessing, the system evaluates 
frame quality by computing statistical indicators such as 
illumination metrics and weather-related features. Frames 
that do not meet confidence thresholds are filtered out to 
avoid incorrect lane estimation.The refined frames are then 
passed through an attention-enhanced feature extraction 
module that identifies lane boundaries using convolutional 
neural networks and spatial attention mechanisms. Multiscale feature aggregation and temporal refinement modules 
analyze both near-field and far-field lane structures while 
maintaining frame-to-frame continuity.Finally, geometric 
constraint optimization is applied to model lane boundaries 
using curvature-aware polynomial regression. Adaptive 
threshold learning dynamically calibrates detection 
confidence based on environmental conditions before 
producing the final lane visibility prediction.
B. Data Preprocessing and Adaptive Normalization
 The first stage of the proposed methodology prepares raw 
camera frames for reliable lane detection. Raw images 
captured from the monocular camera often suffer from 
distortions caused by lens imperfections, lighting variations, 
and environmental noise.
To address these issues, the preprocessing module performs 
camera calibration correction and geometric undistortion to
align the captured frames with real-world road geometry. 
Adaptive contrast normalization techniques such as 
histogram equalization are applied to mitigate variations 
caused by rain, shadows, and twilight conditions.
Noise filtering and edge-preserving smoothing are also 
implemented to enhance lane boundary clarity while 
maintaining important structural information. These 
preprocessing steps ensure that the subsequent feature 
extraction module receives illumination-normalized and 
geometrically consistent image inputs
C. Attention Enhanced – Lane Feature Extraction
 The second stage of the framework focuses on 
identifying lane boundaries from the preprocessed frames 
using deep learning techniques. A lightweight convolutional 
neural network integrated with spatial and channel attention 
mechanisms is used to extract discriminative lane features.
The attention mechanism dynamically emphasizes relevant 
lane structures while suppressing background noise such as 
roadside objects, vehicles, or shadows. Multi-scale feature 
aggregation is employed to capture both near-field and 
distant lane markings, allowing the system to estimate 
longer visibility ranges.
The output of this module consists of probabilistic lane 
boundary representations that indicate the likely position of 
lane markings in the image. These representations are then 
forwarded to the geometric optimization module for precise 
visibility estimation.
D. Geometric Constraint Optimization and 
Visibility Enhancement
 After lane boundaries are detected, the system converts 
the extracted lane features into measurable visibility 
distances. The geometric optimization module performs 
curvature-aware polynomial regression to model lane 
boundaries accurately even on curved or serpentine 
roads.Unlike traditional approaches that assume straight lane 
boundaries, the proposed method incorporates geometric 
constraints that adapt to road curvature and terrain variations. 
The algorithm evaluates probabilistic lane confidence values 
and dynamically calibrates detection thresholds using 
adaptive statistical modeling.The lane visibility distance is 
calculated by converting the detected lane length in pixel 
space into real-world measurements using camera calibration 
parameters:

This dynamic estimation process allows the system to 
extend beyond fixed visibility limits and provide reliable 
measurements across different road environments.
E. Adaptive Threshold Learning
 Traditional lane detection systems rely on static 
thresholds for filtering lane boundary candidates, which often
leads to reduced accuracy under varying weather conditions. 
To address this limitation, the proposed framework 
implements adaptive threshold learning based on frame-level 
statistical metrics.The system dynamically adjusts linearity 
scores and confidence thresholds using probabilistic 
calibration methods derived from environmental indicators 
such as illumination levels and road surface conditions. This 
adaptive mechanism improves detection stability and reduces 
the number of missing or unreliable frames during adverse 
weather.
F. Visibility Prediction And Output
 In the final stage, the optimized lane boundaries and 
calibrated thresholds are used to compute the maximum 
reliable lane visibility distance for each frame. The output of 
the system is the predicted visibility distance in meters along 
with confidence indicators derived from probabilistic 
modeling.These visibility estimates can be integrated into 
Advanced Driver Assistance Systems (ADAS) to support 
lane-keeping assistance, driver alerts, and road safety 
analytics. The proposed framework is designed to operate 
efficiently on CPU-based systems without requiring 
expensive sensors such as LiDAR, making it suitable for 
scalable deployment in intelligent transportation systems.
IV.RESEULT AND DISCUSION
The results obtained from the proposed camera-based lane 
visibility estimation framework demonstrate its capability to 
analyze lane visibility distance across different road 
environments and weather conditions using the Slovakia lane 
visibility dataset. The system processes road video frames 
captured from urban and suburban roads using a monocular 
camera and estimates the maximum visible lane distance 
using computer vision and geometric modeling techniques.
The experimental evaluation indicates that road infrastructure 
and environmental conditions significantly influence lane 
visibility distance. Highway environments such as D1 and D2 
highways provide clearer lane markings and straighter road 
structures, allowing longer visibility distances. In contrast, 
urban roads present additional challenges due to 
intersections, occlusions, vehicle congestion, and worn-out 
lane markings.Weather conditions also play a crucial role in 
determining detection reliability. Rain and low-light 
environments reduce the contrast between lane markings and 
the road surface, which decreases the number of analyzable 
frames. Despite these challenges, the proposed system 
demonstrates the ability to estimate lane visibility distance 
effectively using only a monocular camera setup. These 
results confirm that a low-cost camera-based system can 
support real-time road safety analysis and Advanced Driver 
Assistance Systems (ADAS)
A. Experimental Setup
 The proposed lane visibility estimation framework was 
implemented using Python 3.10 and computer vision 
libraries such as OpenCV, NumPy, and Tensorflow for 
image processing and feature extraction.
Experiments were conducted on a workstation equipped with:
• Intel Core i7 processor
• 16 GB RAM
• NVIDIA GTX 1660 GPU
The evaluation was performed using the Slovakia Lane 
Visibility Dataset, which contains road video recordings 
captured from urban and suburban roads in Slovakia under 
varying weather conditions such as clear weather, rain, and 
low-light environments. The dataset includes highway 
segments such as D1 and D2 highways, as well as urban road 
environments containing intersections and curved road 
segments. Each video sequence was divided into frames, and 
preprocessing operations such as camera calibration, noise 
filtering, and contrast normalization were applied before 
performing lane detection and visibility estimation.
B. Performance Evaluation Metrics
 The performance of the proposed system was evaluated 
using the following metrics:
• Lane Visibility Distance (meters) – Maximum 
measurable lane distance in each frame
• Analyzable Frame Ratio (%) – Percentage of frames 
where lane detection is successful
• Lane Boundary Linearity Score (LBLS) – Measures 
how closely lane boundaries follow linear structures
• Average Lane Linearity Score (ALLS) – Average 
detection confidence across frames

for camera-based systems.
2.Weather Impact On Lane Detection
Weather conditions significantly affect lane detection 
reliability. Rain and low-light conditions reduce the 
visibility of lane markings due to reflections, water 
accumulation, and reduced illumination.Experimental 
observations indicate that during clear weather conditions, 
the system successfully analyzed up to 86.8% of frames, 
while during rainy conditions, the analyzable frame ratio 
dropped to approximately 42.6%.These results highlight the 
need for adaptive image preprocessing and contrast 
enhancement techniques in camera-based lane detection 
systems.
3.Lane Detection Reliability Across Road Environment
 The reliability of lane detection also varies depending on 
the type of road environment. Highways provide relatively 
consistent lane structures and fewer obstacles, allowing 
higher detection reliability. Experimental results show that 
the D2 highway achieved the highest analysable frame 
ratio of 86.8%, followed by the D1 highway with 77.3%, 
while urban roads showed significantly lower reliability at 
47.4%.The lower performance in urban environments is 
primarily caused by occlusions from vehicles, pedestrian
crossings, road curvature, and faded lane markings.
V. DISCUSSION
The experimental results demonstrate that camera-based 
lane visibility estimation systems can provide meaningful 
visibility measurements in real-world driving environments. 
Highway roads consistently provide higher visibility 
distances due to clearer lane markings and simpler road 
geometry. In contrast, urban roads present significant 
challenges due to complex road layouts and environmental 
occlusions. Weather conditions such as rain and low 
illumination significantly reduce detection reliability, 
emphasizing the importance of adaptive image 
preprocessing and feature extraction techniques. Overall, the 
proposed lane visibility estimation framework proves that 
monocular camera systems can be used effectively for lane 
visibility analysis without relying on expensive sensors such 
as LiDAR. This makes the approach suitable for scalable 
deployment in intelligent transportation systems and 
advanced driver assistance applications.
 VI. Conclusion
This research presented an advanced camera-based 
framework for estimating lane visibility distance on urban 
and suburban roadways using monocular vision systems. 
The proposed system introduces an adaptive hybrid 
approach that integrates attention-enhanced feature 
extraction, dynamic threshold learning, and geometric 
constraint optimization to improve the reliability of lane 
visibility estimation under varying environmental 
conditions. Unlike traditional single-frame geometric 
models that rely on fixed linearity thresholds, the proposed 
Adaptive Fusion Net framework dynamically adjusts 
detection parameters according to contextual and 
environmental variations, thereby improving robustness 
across diverse road scenarios. The experimental evaluation 
based on the Slovakia road dataset demonstrates that lane 
visibility distance varies significantly across different road 
classes and weather conditions. Highway environments 
generally provide higher measurable visibility due to clearer 
lane markings and simpler road geometry, while urban
environments introduce additional challenges such as 
intersections, occlusions, and curved road segments. 
Adverse weather conditions such as rain, snow, and twilight 
also affect lane detection reliability by reducing the number 
of analysable frames and altering visual contrast in captured 
images. The results confirm that a hybrid computer vision 
framework combining deep feature extraction with 
geometric modelling can significantly improve lane 
detection stability and visibility estimation accuracy 
compared to conventional approaches. Furthermore, the 
proposed system maintains computational efficiency by 
operating on standard CPU-based hardware without 
requiring expensive sensors such as LiDAR or stereo vision 
systems. This makes the framework suitable for large-scale 
deployment in Advanced Driver Assistance Systems 
(ADAS) and intelligent transportation infrastructures. 
Overall, the proposed Adaptive fusion Net-based visibility 
estimation system provides a scalable and cost-effective 
solution for real-time road visibility analysis. Future work 
will focus on improving the robustness of the framework 
under extreme environmental conditions such as fog, heavy 
rain, and sun glare. Additional research will also explore 
temporal deep learning architectures and domain adaptation 
techniques to further enhance model generalization across 
different geographic regions and road infrastructures.
